The goal of this posting is to demonstrate a procedure to split CMB data into subsets and comparing their angles which will serve as an important consistency check on the as-measured polarization angles during unblinding.

Cross-checking RPS Polarization Angles

The angle we get from a given EB power spectrum (in the absence of birefringence) comes from the fact that the polarization angles we use to make timestreams differ from those we use to make maps. The real BICEP3 CMB data as it current exists uses some generally ideal values for the polarization angles of the detectors when we turn timestreams into maps. Having no hard prior on what to set them to, the polarization angles are set to the same value. On the other hand, the angles I measured with the RPS show a detector-to-detector scatter of ~0.5 deg.

That means that I should be able to split our real coadded maps into subsets based on the angle we expect from our RPS-derived polarization angles and then compare the difference in the angle between the different subsets. Since we're looking only at relative differences, we keep ourselves blind to the absolute birefringence angle. While it doesn't really give us information on the overall angle, having a confirmation that I got the relative angles correct lends confidence that there isn't something glaringly wrong in the pol angles I measured using the RPS.

The angle we fit to the real data as it currently exists will be a combination of the miscalibration angle of the instrument and any celestial isotropic polarization signals

\begin{equation} \alpha_\text{obs} = \alpha_\text{B3} + \alpha_\text{IPR} \end{equation}

If we split the data into subsets grouped by angle and subtract the angles fit from those subsets, we are left with only a difference between the instrument angles as the celestial signals should cancel-out.

\begin{equation} \begin{split} \Delta\alpha &= \alpha_{\text{obs},i} - \alpha_{\text{obs},j}\\ &= \alpha_{\text{B3},i} + \alpha_\text{IPR} - \alpha_{\text{B3},j} - \alpha_\text{IPR}\\ &= \alpha_{\text{B3},i} - \alpha_{\text{B3},j}\\ \end{split} \end{equation}

The figure below shows how the data could be split up. The choice is relatively arbitrary, but limited ultimately by the fact that $\sigma_\alpha$ increases with fewer detectors. In this case, I choose to split the pairs evenly into thirds where the mean difference in global rotation angle between the lower-to-middle and middle-to-upper datasets is expected to be ~0.3°.

I created three subsets of B18 sims, 10 realizations each, based on the detector grouping above. I then fit the global rotation angle to the EB spectra of each subset and compare the those angles to the angles I expect. Because I have to make the individual years of 2016 through 2018 in order to make the three year coadd, I fit angles to the three years individually as well as the B18 coadded maps.

In the figure below, I show results the unlensed-CMB signal (type 2) and Lense-CMB+Noise+Dust (Type 8), type 2 to look at the subsets without noise and type 8 because that's what we'll ultimately be comparing the real CMB data to. I also include an option for selecting between fitting over our typical 9 bandpowers or fitting our 9 plus higher ells.

The pager below shows the resulting differences of the angles.

If we imagine a scenario where the angles we currently have in the FP data file are actually correct, then the difference angles would all be zero, which is a case we would hope to cause a failure of these tests. In the table below, I show the resulting pte's for such a case using the L-LCDM+N+D (Type-8) using our typical 9 bandpowers where the PTE's are calculated as:

\begin{equation} \text{PTE} = \left.1-\text{normcdf}\left(\frac{\Delta\alpha_\text{real}-\left<\Delta\alpha_{\text{sims}}\right>}{\sigma_{\Delta\alpha,\text{sims}}}\right)\right|_{\Delta\alpha,\text{real}=0} \end{equation}

Where a PTE<0.5 implies $\Delta\alpha_\text{real}\lt\left<\Delta\alpha_\text{sims}\right>$ and PTE>0.5 for $\Delta\alpha_\text{real}\gt\left<\Delta\alpha_\text{sims}\right>$.

Only the low-minus-high Δα values fail according to our typical requirements (0.01 < PTE < 0.99).

I also show a table for fits that include 14 bandpowers. Other values become marginal with the increased constraining power, but the low-minus-high value is still the only one that I would confidently say provides a sufficient failure.
