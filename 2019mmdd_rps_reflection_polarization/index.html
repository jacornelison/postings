Effect of Polarization Angle Estimates due to Differential Polarization Reflection of Aluminum

2023 Jan 20 — J. Cornelison

In this posting, I calculate the effect of differential polarization rotation on polarization angles due to reflecting off of a surface of aluminum and develop a method for correcting for it.

Complex Refractive Index of Aluminum

The complex index of refraction of a medium is given by

\begin{equation} \underline{n} = n+ik \end{equation}

Where

\begin{equation} \begin{split} &n = \sqrt{\frac{\left|\underline{\varepsilon}\right|+\varepsilon}{2}}\\ &k = \sqrt{\frac{\left|\underline{\varepsilon}\right|-\varepsilon}{2}}\\ \end{split} \end{equation}

and $\varepsilon$ is the complex dielectric permittivity.

\begin{equation} \varepsilon = \frac{2\sigma}{f}i \end{equation}

Where $f$ is the frequency of the photon of interest and $\sigma$ is the conductance.

I calculate the conductance of Aluminum $\sigma$ as a function of temperature $T$ using a model given in Eq. 24 in this paper

\begin{equation} \sigma(T) = A\left(\frac{A}{\sigma_0(T)}+\rho_0\right)^{-1} \end{equation} Where \begin{equation} \sigma_0(T) = C\cdot\left(\frac{\theta}{T}\right)^5\left(\int_{0}^{\theta/T}\frac{x^5}{cosh(x)-1}\right)^{-1} \end{equation}

and $\theta$ is the Debye temperature of Aluminum, $\rho_0$ is some residual resistance and $A$ and $C$ are emperically derived coefficients.

the index of refraction for Aluminum is $$ n_{Al}(90GHz,240K) = 2200(1+i) $$

Calculating the Reflection Coeffecients

To calculate the new adjusted polarization angle, we start with the set of basis vectors which define the detector pointing $\left[\hat{D}_1,\,\hat{D}_2,\,\hat{D}_3\right]$, the basis vector which describes the co-polar axis $\hat{D}_p$ (See , §II), and the vector which describes the mirror normal $\hat{N}_{mirr}$.

We first calculate the reflection coefficients for the S- and P-components which are a function of the incident angle $\theta_{inc}$ and the refractive index of aluminum

\begin{equation} \theta_{inc} = \cos^{-1}\left(\hat{N}_{mirr}\cdot-\hat{D}_3\right) \end{equation}

Note: that $\hat{D}_3$ is negative because it normally points into the mirror and we need to calculate the angle with it pointing out of the mirror.

And the coefficients are calculated as

\begin{equation} \begin{split} &r_S = \frac{n_{air}\cos\theta_{inc}-n_{al}\sqrt{1-\left(\frac{n_{air}}{n_{al}}\sin\theta_{inc}\right)}}{n_{air}\cos\theta_{inc}+n_{al}\sqrt{1-\left(\frac{n_{air}}{n_{al}}\sin\theta_{inc}\right)}}\\ &r_P = \frac{n_{air}\sqrt{1-\left(\frac{n_{air}}{n_{al}}\sin\theta_{inc}\right)}-n_{al}\cos\theta_{inc}}{n_{air}\sqrt{1-\left(\frac{n_{air}}{n_{al}}\sin\theta_{inc}\right)}+n_{al}\cos\theta_{inc}}\\ \end{split} \end{equation}

During the calculations, $n_{air}=1$ and $n_{al}=2200(1+i)$ as described above. The reflection coefficients as a function of incident angle are plotted below.

Calculating Pol Angles Due to Differential Reflection

We first describe the plane of incidence by calculating its normal which is the cross between the detector pointing and the mirror normal:

\begin{equation} \hat{N}_{inc} = \frac{\hat{D}_3\times\hat{N}_{mirr}}{\left|\hat{D}_3\times\hat{N}_{mirr}\right|} \end{equation}

We then calculate the vectors describing the S- and P- components. The S-component is the projection of the co-polar axis onto the axis orthogonal to the plane of incidence (i.e. $\hat{N}_{inc}$). The P-component is just the part of the co-polar axis that isn't projected onto $\hat{N}_{inc}$:

\begin{equation} \begin{split} &V_S = \left(\hat{D}_P\cdot\hat{N}_{inc}\right)\hat{N}_{inc}\\ &V_P = \hat{D}_P - V_S \\ \end{split} \end{equation}

We then apply the reflection coefficients to the S- and P-components and add them back together to get the rotated co-polar axis vector $\tilde{D}_P$

\begin{equation} \tilde{D}_P = r_S V_S + r_P V_P \end{equation}

Then we can convert $\tilde{D}_P$ into a polarization angle working backwards from Eq. 6 in

\begin{equation}\label{newangle} \tilde{\phi}_P = \tan^{-1}\left(\frac{\tilde{D}_P\cdot-\hat{D}_2}{\tilde{D}_P\cdot\hat{D}_1}\right) \end{equation}

It's useful to calculate the "pair-diff" polarization angle between an A and B detector pair which, when we have no cross-polar response, is simplified to

\begin{equation} \phi_{pair} \sim \frac{\phi_A+\left(\phi_B-90^\circ\right)}{2} \end{equation}

The figure below shows the level of bias for input angles of 0° and 90° and for different distances away from the focal plane as a function of DK angle. As we can see, the level of bias on the polarization angle can get quite high depending on the deck angle and the effect is equal and opposite for the orthogonal polarization state. So while it is significant when just looking at pol A or Pol B, the effect cancels out in the pair-difference signal.

Correcting for Differential Reflection

Given that this effect is physical, we should correct for it. Given a knowledge of the pointing model and given some measured polarization angle $\tilde{D}_P$ that involves reflecting off of the FFFlat (from measurements with the RPS, say) we can start at Eq. \ref{newangle} and work backwards to solve for $\hat{D}_P$ and convert into an angle.

We can write the projections of $\tilde{D}_P$ onto the plane of incidence

\begin{equation} \tilde{D}_P = \tilde{V}_S+\tilde{V}_P \end{equation}

Where

\begin{equation} \begin{split} &\tilde{V}_S = (\tilde{D}_P\cdot\hat{N}_{inc})\hat{N}_{inc}\\ &\tilde{V}_P = \tilde{D}_P-\tilde{V}_S\\ \end{split} \end{equation}

then

\begin{equation} \hat{D}_P = \frac{\tilde{V}_S}{r_S}+\frac{\tilde{V}_P}{r_P} \end{equation}

And we convert that back into an angle with Eq: \ref{newangle}.
