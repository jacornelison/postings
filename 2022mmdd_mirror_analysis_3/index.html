2022 Moon Analysis III: Moon Phase Correction

2022 Jun 29 — J. Cornelison

In my previous posting , I walked through the procedure of modelling the phases of the moon and estimating an offset in Azimuth and Elevation resulting from convolving our beams with a non-uniform brightness temperature profiles of the moon. In this posting, I apply a moon phase correction to the beam centroids estimated from beam maps of the moon. I then fit for mirror parameters with and without this phase correction per-DK and examine how those corrections improve the results by looking at the best-fit residuals. I report overall best-fit mirror parameters of $Tilt=44.88^\circ\pm0.022^\circ$ and $Roll=-0.07^\circ\pm0.025^\circ$ with an apparent scaling and rotation of the focal plane of $0.998\pm0.001$ and $0.06^\circ\pm0.01^\circ$ respectively. These results will be applied to the pointing model for the RPS data which was taken in the 2021/2022 Austral Summer.

Moon Phase Correction

Moon data is grouped into "observations" which typically consist of three consecutive 1-hour GCP schedules taken at a single DK-angle and generally maps the beams for entire focal plane. Each 1-hour schedule is made up of nineteen 9°Az-by-2°El scans with the first scan starting at -14°El offset from the Moon's center and incrementing by +1.5°El with each consecutive scan. In each observation, I acquire beam centroids by fitting 2D Gaussians to beam maps in raw mount coordinates before converting to instrument-fixed coordinates.

On a scan-by-scan basis, I estimate a Moon phase correction factor for both Azimuth and Elevation and apply them to the best-fit beam centroids using the modelling procedure outlined in §2. I then fit for mirror parameters per-observation using the method described in §2.

In the pager below, I show the best-fit Tilt and Roll parameters vs observation time with and without this phase correction. I also show the Az/El offsets predicted by the moon-phase model in Fig 1.2 for comparison. The other X-axis parameters in the pager probe for systematics in either DK angle or diurnal fluctuations for the "time-of-day" selection. I color-code the data based on the distinct 24-hour periods in which the data was collected.

The mirror parameters after phase correction shift as expected: the mirror angles have an extra factor of 2 from reflection, so any shift in the Moon's Az/El should roughly correspond to half that shift in mirror roll/tilt respectively. However, we can see in the pager that this isn't the full story. We can still see statistically significant offsets in average roll for a given observing period. However, we don't see any dependence on DK angle or any evidence of diurnal heating.

Beam Center Residuals

Example Residuals

This next section takes a closer look at the beam center residuals to probe for systematics. For a quick reference, in this next figure I show how individual errors on a given mirror or source parameter affect the residuals.

Most of these are pretty straightforward; A mis-estimation of the Moon Az/El or mirror tilt to any significant degree will cause an apparent translation in the residuals. The mirror roll, however, will also induce an off-axis roll in the residuals which can change the apparent orientation of the focal plane. In the context of polarization calibration, this last point is particularly important to us so we pay extra attention to the roll parameter.

The first thing to note is that the statistical uncertainty on the beam center fits is very good $\sigma\leq0.005^\circ$. I only plot detectors whose pair was also captured. As such almost every arrow actually represents two independent measurements of the pointing in that location. Additionally, there are several observations that were taken at the same DK angle within a couple hours of each other and are also in good agreement. this is more easily seen in the difference histograms in Figure A.1 in the Appendix. What this means though it is that we're capturing coherent systematics somewhere in the pointing with very high signal to noise. There's some other underlying fluctuations, but the dominant structure we see across the focal plane appears to be an underscaling of the focal plane which changes in severity over time.

FPU Scaling and Orientation

If I average over all observations, the scaling becomes more evident. I estimate a scaling of the focal plane by applying a scaling to the Moon-derived centroids that further minimizes the residuals between CMB-derived beam centroids and the Moon-derived beam centroids assuming a mirror tilt and roll fixed at $44.88^\circ$ and $-0.07^\circ$ respectively. Additionally, I fit an angle because, in the context of polarization calibration, we care mostly about the apparent orientation of the focal plane as that sets the point to which we reference the polarization angles.

\begin{equation} \begin{split} x &= A\left(x_{0}\cos\zeta-y_{0}\sin\zeta\right)\\ y &= A\left(x_{0}\sin\zeta+y_{0}\cos\zeta\right)\\ \end{split} \end{equation}

Then using CMB-derived beam centers as the 'observed' quantity, the chi-square to minimize is just:

\begin{equation} \chi^2=\sum \frac{(x_{CMB,i}-x_{i})^2+(y_{CMB,i}-y_{i})^2}{\sigma_i^2} \end{equation}

I find an overall scaling of $0.998\pm0.001$ and FPU rotation angle of $0.06\pm0.01^\circ$ and plot the beam-center residuals with the angle and scaling applied in the pager below.

I also perform the fits per-observation an plot them as a function of time-of-day and DK angle below. Other than distinct offsets at the different observing periods, there are obvious dependencies on DK or time.

Results and Discussion

While there are still a few questions to address in this particular analysis, I've made a table below reporting on the mirror parameters that I'll use in the RPS analysis along with quoted uncertainty which I'll use in an error propagation analysis. I calculate the statistical error as the standard deviation of the difference in mirror parameters between observations at the same DK angles that were taken within an hour of each other. There's two significant contributors to the systematic error: the scatter across the data within the three 24-hour observing periods and the scatter across the entire dataset. I calculate the systematic uncertainty by adding in quadrature the standard deviations of the of Obs-to-Obs scatter per-24-hour period and the largest difference between the means of those 24-hour periods.

I find both long- and short-timescale fluctuating structure across the focal plane that appears to average down to an overall scaling of $0.998\pm0.001$ and a rotation of the focal plane of $0.06\pm0.01^\circ$. The next step is to look at the RPS-derived centroids and see if it has similar features as seen in the Moon data and simply subtract off the additional focal plane rotation from the polarization angle estimates. If not, we may have to include the difference in FPU angles as an additional systematic uncertainty.
