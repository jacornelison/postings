First

We first want to derive a generic equation that gives the apparent elevation $El'$ as a function of the detector pointing $(r,\,\theta)$, the mirror tilt $\alpha_{tilt}$ and the elevation $El$.

The mirror comoves with the receiver so we reference comoving angles to the boresight where $r=0$ which I'm just calling the $r_0$-axis and the axis at which $r=90^\circ$ the $r_{90}$-axis. In practice, we install the mirror such that the rays are redirected out the "back", so I've constructed the diagram below to reflect that.

Then from the diagram, we can see that the apparent elevation is \begin{equation} El' = \beta_{ref} + \alpha_{norm} - (El - 90^\circ) \end{equation}

$\alpha_{tilt}$ is defined such that when $\alpha_{tilt}=0$, $\beta_{inc}=0$ at the boresight. $\alpha_{norm}$ is the angle measured from the $r_{90}$-axis to the axis normal to the mirror, counter-clockwise as seen in the diagram. This is related to the mirror tilt by:

\begin{equation} \alpha_{norm} = \alpha_{tilt}-90 \end{equation}

where $\beta_{ref}$ is the angle of the reflected ray WRT the mirror normal. Since the mirror is flat, we know that the reflected angle is equal to the incident angle $\beta_{ref}=\beta_{inc}$. We can find $\beta_{inc}$ by exploiting the fact that internal triangle angles sum to 180° and noting that a triangle is created by the intersections of the detector ray (in red), the $r_{90}$-axis, and the mirror.

\begin{equation} 180^\circ = \alpha_{tilt}+(90^\circ-r \cos\theta)+(90^\circ-\beta_{inc}) \end{equation} Solving for $\beta_{inc}$ substituting for $\beta_{ref}$ gives \begin{equation} \beta_{ref} = \alpha_{tilt}-r \cos \theta \end{equation} We can then rewrite equation 2 in terms of $r$, $\theta$, and $\alpha_{tilt}$. \begin{equation} El' = 2\alpha_{tilt}-r\cos\theta - El \end{equation}
