We first want to derive a generic equation that gives the apparent elevation $El'$ as a function of the detector pointing $(r,\,\theta)$, the mirror tilt $\alpha_{tilt}$ and the elevation $El$.

The mirror comoves with the receiver so we reference comoving angles to the boresight where $r=0$ which I'm just calling the $r_0$-axis and the axis at which $r=90^\circ$ the $r_{90}$-axis.

$\alpha_{tilt}$ is defined such that when $\alpha_{tilt}=0$, $\beta_{inc}=0$ at the boresight. $\alpha_{norm}$ is the angle measured from the $r_{90}$-axis to the axis normal to the mirror, counter-clockwise as seen in the diagram. This is related to the mirror tilt by:

\begin{equation} \alpha_{norm} = \alpha_{tilt}-90 \end{equation} Them from the diagram, we can see that the apparent elevation is \begin{equation} El' = El + \beta_{ref} + \alpha_{norm} \end{equation} where $\beta_{ref}$ is the angle of the reflected ray WRT the mirror normal. Since the mirror is flat, we know that the reflected angle is equal to the incident angle $\beta_{ref}=\beta_{inc}$. We can find $\beta_{inc}$ by exploiting the fact that internal triangle angles sum to 180° and noting that a triangle is created by the intersections of the detector ray (in red), the $r_{90}$-axis, and the mirror. \begin{equation} 180^\circ = \alpha_{tilt}+(90^\circ-r)+(90^\circ-\beta_{inc}) \end{equation} Solving for $\beta_{inc}$ substituting for $\beta_{ref}$ gives \begin{equation} \beta_{ref} = \alpha_{tilt}-r \end{equation} We can then rewrite equation 2 in terms of $r$ and $\alpha_{tilt}$. \begin{equation} El' = El+2\alpha_{tilt}-r-90^\circ \end{equation}
