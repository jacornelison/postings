This posting is part of a series which comprises the IPR analysis, see previous postings for more on this.

In this posting, I examine the effect on α and $\siga$ due to the fact that we didn't measure the pol angles for all detector pairs used in B18. In addition to increased noise from simply having fewer detectors on-sky, we are also worried about how well the standard B18 purification matrix will work on a map with reduced coverage. Fortunately, I find that the matrix purification shows an increase in $\siga$ by only 3-5% for maps with reduced coverages. We do, however, see an increase in $\siga$ of 30% resulting from increased noise due to having less detectors. I find that if we include higher ℓ-bins, we might be able to counter this effect though.

Purification of B18 with fewer detectors

Making purification matrices is a really involved process so we'd rather not have to remake them for this analysis. Instead, we're hoping to reuse the purification matrix created for the BICEP3 3-year dataset (AKA B18) that was used in BK18.

For this analysis, we will only be using detector pairs for which we have high-quality measurements from the RPS. This means we have only ~70% of the the detector pairs that were used in any of the given years comprising B18. Because fewer detectors means less coverage, we can expect a hit to the efficiency of the matrix purification in the maps which only contain detectors with RPS-derived pol angles.

I check the impact on the purification efficiency by comparing two B18 simsets of 10 realizations, one with maps comprising all detectors used in B18 (sernum 6600, B18-rerun) and another with only detectors that also have RPS-derived angles (sernum 6622, B18-rerun-subset). Some notes:

  • Each bandpower in the plot is the average over the 10 realizations. Both simsets use realization indices of 1-10 so, because we use seeded realizations, the spectra would be identical if using less detectors had no effect.
  • I show both the spectra themselves and the fractional difference between the two. For the frac-diff plots, I limit the y-axis ±1 where needed as large deviations beyond 1 are due to a small number being divided by another small number.
  • As with my recent previous postings, I cross Type 8 (Lensed Signal+Noise+Dust) T/E/B with a given signal's T/E/B. This is useful because it gives meaning to EB/TB angle fits for noise and dust signal types. For instance, we know that $\sigasq$ scales linearly with BB-power with signals crossed with CMB so we can predict a priori through the spectra how $\siga$ will be affected by this reduction in coverage or any inefficiencies in purification. All other signals are crossed with type 8 to make the spectra comparable in an absolute sense.

Differences in TT, TE, and EE due to coverage are on the percent level for the most part -- perhaps not too surprising. When we matrix purify, we don't see any change to TE, or EE because we don't bother adding E lost to E→B leakage back into E, I think.

Focusing primarily on BB then:

  • Unlensed-LCDM:Differences in coverage mostly affect the three lowest ℓ-bins, which I think makes intuitive sense. Lower coverage translates into a higher sample variance and the lower ℓ's are most affected by that. When we matrix-purify, matrix purification is qualitatively still effective and most of the large deviations in the frac-diff are due to dividing a small number by another small number.
  • Lensed-LCDM: The comment about coverage is the same as before. However, now that we have a high-SNR measurement of lensing, the difference between purified spectra is only 3% or so, which tells us that the purification matrix isn't running into any issues with the reduced detectors or coverage.
  • Noise: Whether we're purifying or not, we can see the BB-power has increased on the order of 30%. No surprise there -- we've dropped the number of detectors we're using by 30% and the noise BB power is going to reflect that. I don't know that BB-power and N-detectors are known to be strongly linked -- I'd expect pair coverages/weights to obfuscate that relationship a bit -- so the fact that both are ~30% is interesting.
  • L-LCDM+Noise(+Dust): we're noise-dominated in BB at high-ℓ, so we see that 30% increase in BB-power where noise is dominant. At lower ℓ, the noise isn't as dominant over the lensing signal and the contribution to dust is more profound as well, so while we see an increase in BB, it's only 10-20% at those lower ℓ for both type 7 and 8.

In summary, we can see through Lensed-signal only that the purification efficiency for maps with our reduced coverage is around 97%, so we should be good to continue using the B18 purification matrix so long as the angles aren't biased in any way.

How does this affect our angle fits?

The table below shows a comparison of the angle uncertainties between our two simsets. In either case, we do see a ~50% increase in $\siga$.
