2022 Moon Analysis III: Moon Phase Correction

2022 Jun 29 — J. Cornelison

In my previous posting , I walked through the procedure of modelling the phases of the moon and estimating an offset in Azimuth and Elevation resulting from convolving our beams with a non-uniform brightness temperature profiles of the moon. In this posting, I apply a moon phase correction to the beam centroids estimated from beam maps of the moon. I then fit for mirror parameters with and without this phase correction per-DK and examine how those corrections improve the results by looking at the best-fit residuals.

Moon Phase Correction

Moon data is grouped into "observations" which typically consist of three consecutive 1-hour GCP schedules taken at a single DK-angle and generally maps the beams for entire focal plane. Each 1-hour schedule is made up of nineteen 9°Az-by-2°El scans with the first scan starting at -14°El offset from the Moon's center and incrementing by +1.5°El with each consecutive scan. In each observation, I acquire beam centroids by fitting 2D Gaussians to beam maps in raw mount coordinates before converting to instrument-fixed coordinates.

On a scan-by-scan basis, I estimate a Moon phase correction factor for both Azimuth and Elevation and apply them to the best-fit beam centroids using the modelling procedure outlined in §2. I then fit for mirror parameters per-observation using the method described in §2.

In the pager below, I show the best-fit Tilt and Roll parameters vs observation time with and without this phase correction. The pager is also color-coded based on the DK angle at which each observation was taken. I also show the Az/El offsets predicted by the moon-phase model in Fig 1.2.

The mirror parameters after phase correction shift as expected: the mirror angles have an extra factor of 2 from reflection, so any shift in the Moon's Az/El should roughly correspond to half that shift in mirror roll/tilt respectively. However, we can see in the pager that this isn't the full story. We can still see statistically significant offsets in average roll for a given observing period (i.e. the three distinct ~24-hour periods at which moon observations were conducted).

Most of these are pretty straightforward; A mis-estimation of the Moon Az/El or mirror tilt to any significant degree will cause an apparent translation in the residuals.

The pager below arranges the observations (denoted by the DK at which they were taken) in chronological order where "0_1" to "135_1" were taken before the December 4th solar eclipse, "23_2" to "68_4" were taken on 10 Dec (when the moon was low enough in elevation to observe again), and "0_2" to "157" were taken on 25/26 Jan after the completion of the RPS observation period. The "Fit" option shows residuals for a single overall mirror fit and the residuals if I fit the mirror parameters individually. The "Coord" option shows the residuals in instrument-fixed coordinates and a "mirror-fixed" coordinate where I've derotated the DK angle from the residuals to help identify any systematics common to a point on the mirror.

Beside the rotation, I also note that the residuals have both long- and short-timescale features. This is most easily seen when comparing residuals at the same deck angles. For instance, the features in "23_1" and "174_1" are much larger than 23_2/23_3 and 174_2/174_3 that were taken one week later. Further, we can see smaller but still coherent shifts between 23_2 and 23_3 (also 174_2 and 174_3) which were taken within hours of each other which implies these are physical shifts on relatively short timescales.

If I average over all DK angles, the residuals average down for the most part. I fit a rotation and scaling to further minimize the residuals and we see a small amount of scaling start to emerge along with a small overall rotation of the focal plane. While the scaling further reduces the residuals by quite a bit, derotation doesn't appear to have a similarly large effect. The plan is to look into the pointing residuals from the RPS data and see if there's a similar scaling and rotation in the focal plane.

In context to using these mirror parameters for other analyses, I'm thinking the best approach is to use the average parameters here to determine the source location and then, while locking the source location in place, re-fitting for the mirror parameters on a schedule-to-schedule basis.

Results

While there are still a few questions to address in this particular analysis, I've made a table below reporting on the mirror parameters that I'll use in the RPS analysis along with quoted uncertainty which I'll use in an error propagation analysis. I calculate the statistical error as the mean of the normalized errors returned by matmin.m during the mirror fitting procedure. There's two significant contributors to the systematic error: the DK-dependent scatter and the scatter on the mean over the three 24-hour observing periods. I calculate the systematic uncertainty by adding in quadrature the standard deviations of the of DK-to-DK scatter per-24-hour period and the largest difference between the means of those 24-hour periods.
